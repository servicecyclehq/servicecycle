# Renewal-management practice primer

Use this when a question has a "why" or "what's the right approach" shape rather than a "where is X" shape. These are stable practitioner conventions — not ServiceCycle-specific terminology, though ServiceCycle's data model reflects them.

## The notice cadence — three dates that matter

- **Renewal date** (`endDate`) — when the contract expires and the new term begins (or terminates if not renewed).
- **Notice / cancel-by date** — the last day to give written notice if you want to cancel an auto-renewing contract. This is `endDate` minus the contract's notice period (commonly 30, 60, 90, or 180 days). ServiceCycle stores this as `cancelByDate`.
- **Review-by date** — when you should *start* working the renewal. Higher-value contracts deserve a longer runway. ServiceCycle tiers this automatically: by default 180 days for $100K+, 90 days for $25K–$100K, 30 days for under $25K (60 days when there's no cost data). An admin can retune those breakpoints and lead times in Settings → General → Evaluation Lead Times.

The most common renewal mistake is treating these three as one date or sequencing them wrong. Cancel-by is the door closing — miss it and the contract auto-renews. Review-by is when you start — miss it and you arrive at cancel-by with no leverage. Renewal date is when the new term begins, but by then negotiation is already done.

Practitioner-conventional alert pattern: fire reminders at 90 / 60 / 30 days before each significant date. ServiceCycle's defaults reflect this.

## Auto-renewal and the trap

Auto-renewal (also called "evergreen") clauses bind the customer to a new term unless they give notice within the cancel-by window. Standard in most enterprise SaaS contracts. The trap: cancel-by is often *months* before the contract expires, and missing it by a single day extends the contract for another full term — sometimes with an undisclosed price escalation.

Three things to check on every contract with `autoRenewal=true`:

1. **Notice period.** Is `autoRenewalNoticeDays` set correctly from the contract's actual language? Many managers default to 60 or 90 days but the contract may specify something else.
2. **Method of notice.** Some contracts require written notice via specific channels (registered mail, vendor portal, email to a specific address). ServiceCycle doesn't enforce this — but the negotiation log is the right place to record method and date.
3. **Price escalation on auto-renewal.** Read the renewal pricing clause. Some auto-renew at the same price; others auto-renew at "current list price" (vendor-controlled); others have an explicit annual uplift cap. `annualUpliftPercent` captures whatever the contract specifies — use it.

The auto-renewal trap panel on the Dashboard surfaces every contract with an active auto-renewal clause and its cancel-by countdown. Morning-coffee panel.

## True-ups and license utilization

A **true-up** is the periodic reconciliation between what the customer is licensed for and what they're actually using. Annual at renewal is typical for enterprise agreements (Microsoft EA, Adobe ETLA); named-user SaaS often trues-up monthly through the bill.

True-ups go either direction. **Over-licensed:** paid for more seats than used ("shelfware"). **Under-licensed:** using more than paid for, vendor will bill (sometimes retroactively).

ServiceCycle's license utilization fields (`seatsLicensed`, `seatsActivelyInUse`, computed estimated savings) are where this is recorded. The Contract Detail page shows the utilization % bar. When `seatsActivelyInUse` is materially below `seatsLicensed`, the renewal is the right moment to right-size.

Ask "how many seats are actually being used" *before* the renewal alert fires.

## Co-terming and consolidated negotiation

**Co-terming** is aligning multiple contracts with the same vendor — or across related vendors — to a single renewal date. Combined volume is leverage. Negotiating five Microsoft contracts as one event with $500K total budget gets a better outcome than negotiating five $100K contracts independently.

Two surfaces in ServiceCycle: vendor-level complexity (`Vendor.cotermComplexity` + `cotermNotes`) describes how this vendor handles co-terms in general; contract-level grouping (`Contract.coTermGroup`) is a free-text identifier shared across contracts that should be negotiated together. The `/coterm-summary` endpoint shows combined renewal value for each group.

When a renewal alert fires for one contract in a group, look at the rest of the group too.

## Escalation clauses and CPI caps

An **escalation clause** specifies how price changes at renewal or year-over-year. Common patterns:

- **Fixed annual uplift** — e.g., 5% per year. Predictable.
- **CPI-indexed** — tied to a published Consumer Price Index measure. Variable.
- **CPI or fixed, whichever is greater** (sometimes capped at a ceiling) — vendor-favorable when inflation is high.
- **Greater of CPI or N%** — same shape.
- **Renew at then-current list price** — vendor-controlled. Avoid in negotiation; if you can't, get a multi-year price lock.

`annualUpliftPercent` captures whatever the contract specifies. Budget Forecast uses it to project next year's spend. Negotiation log is where rationale and vendor response get recorded.

## Minimum commits and consumption traps

A **minimum commit** is a contractually-required minimum spend or volume over the term. Common in cloud and consumption-based SaaS. The trap: if usage falls below the commit, the customer pays for the unused portion anyway.

At renewal: what's the actual usage trend? Is there flexibility on the commit pattern (annual minimums with quarterly true-up beat a single end-of-term cliff)? What does the vendor's discount curve look like (a bigger commit unlocks deeper discounts only if you'll use the volume)?

ServiceCycle doesn't have a dedicated minimum-commit field today. Practitioners typically capture commit details in `notes` or a custom field; negotiation log records renewal-time reasoning.

## The value of one consolidated record per vendor

Vendors mutate over time — they're acquired, rebrand, spin up subsidiaries, get listed differently in different procurement systems. The vendor name normalizer (`server/lib/vendorNormalizer.js`) plus the curated alias map exist so a customer with "Microsoft Corporation," "Microsoft," and "MS" in their data ends up with one vendor record.

When a renewal alert fires, you want to see *every* contract with that vendor in one place. Three vendor records for the same vendor → three separate deals when you could have negotiated one. When cleaning up a vendor list, the answer is almost always: pick a canonical name, move the contracts onto it, add the variants as aliases on the canonical record.

## What the renewal brief is actually for

The AI renewal brief at `/contracts/:id` is a structured negotiation summary. Not a substitute for reading the contract, and not a substitute for the practitioner's judgment. It is useful for:

- A 60-second refresher on a contract you haven't touched in months.
- A leverage-points checklist when you're about to pick up the phone with the vendor.
- A handoff document when one renewals manager passes a contract to another.
- A starting point for the negotiation log — copy, edit, augment.

Refresh once new context arrives (fresh utilization read, auto-renewal clause discovery, vendor news item). Don't refresh every time you open the contract — it costs an AI call.
